  • Dental Examinations: Regular dental check-ups involve a comprehensive examination of your oral health, including an assessment of your teeth, gums, tongue, and other oral tissues. Dentists may also perform X-rays to detect any underlying issues.
  • Dental Cleanings: Also known as prophylaxis or scaling, dental cleanings involve the removal of plaque, tartar, and stains from your teeth. This procedure helps prevent tooth decay, gum disease, and bad breath.
  • Fillings: If you have a cavity or tooth decay, the dentist will remove the damaged portion of the tooth and fill the space with a dental filling material, such as composite resin, amalgam, or porcelain. Fillings restore the tooth's function and prevent further decay.
  • Dental Sealants: Sealants are thin plastic coatings applied to the chewing surfaces of the molars and premolars. They provide an extra layer of protection against tooth decay by sealing off the deep grooves and pits where bacteria and food particles can accumulate.
  • Tooth Extractions: In some cases, a tooth may need to be extracted if it is severely decayed, damaged, or causing overcrowding. Dentists can perform simple extractions or refer you to an oral surgeon for more complex cases.
  • Root Canal Therapy: When the pulp inside a tooth becomes infected or inflamed, root canal therapy may be necessary to save the tooth. The dentist removes the infected pulp, cleans the root canal, and seals it with a filling material.
  • Gum Disease Treatment: General dentists diagnose and treat gum disease, also known as periodontal disease. Non-surgical treatments include scaling and root planing (deep cleaning), antibiotic therapy, and recommending improved oral hygiene practices.
  • Oral Cancer Screening: During regular dental check-ups, dentists conduct oral cancer screenings to detect any signs of oral cancer early on. This typically involves a thorough examination of the oral tissues, including the tongue, cheeks, throat, and neck.
  • Oral Health Education: Dentists provide guidance and education on proper oral hygiene practices, including brushing techniques, flossing, diet recommendations, and the importance of regular dental visits.

  • Braces: Braces are the most common orthodontic treatment. They consist of brackets, wires, and bands that apply gentle pressure to gradually move teeth into proper alignment. Braces can address a wide range of orthodontic issues, including crooked teeth, overcrowding, gaps between teeth, and bite problems.
  • Clear Aligners: Clear aligners, such as Invisalign, are an alternative to traditional braces. They are transparent, removable trays that gradually shift teeth into their desired positions. Clear aligners are popular among adults and teenagers who prefer a more discreet orthodontic treatment option.
  • Retainers: After completing orthodontic treatment with braces or aligners, patients usually need to wear retainers. Retainers help maintain the new tooth positions and prevent them from shifting back. There are different types of retainers, including removable retainers and fixed retainers (bonded to the back of the teeth).
  • Orthodontic Consultation: Orthodontists perform thorough evaluations and consultations to assess a patient's orthodontic needs. They examine the teeth, jaws, and facial structure, take X-rays and impressions, and discuss treatment options and expected outcomes.
  • Bite Correction: Orthodontic treatment can correct bite irregularities, such as an overbite (upper front teeth overlap the lower teeth excessively), underbite (lower teeth protrude in front of the upper teeth), crossbite (upper teeth sit inside the lower teeth), or open bite (upper and lower teeth don't meet when biting).
  • Jaw Alignment: Orthodontic treatment may involve addressing jaw discrepancies, such as an overgrown or underdeveloped jaw. Techniques like orthognathic surgery or functional appliances can be used to correct these skeletal issues.
  • Space Maintenance: In cases where primary teeth are lost prematurely or there is insufficient space for permanent teeth to erupt naturally, orthodontists may recommend space maintainers. These devices preserve the space for incoming teeth, preventing crowding and alignment problems.
  • Orthodontic Monitoring: Regular follow-up visits are crucial during orthodontic treatment to monitor progress, make adjustments, and ensure that the treatment plan is on track. Orthodontists use these appointments to assess tooth movement and make any necessary modifications.

  • Teeth Whitening: Teeth whitening is a popular cosmetic procedure that lightens the color of your teeth, removing stains and discoloration. It can be done in-office by a dentist or at home using custom-fitted trays and whitening gel.
  • Dental Veneers: Veneers are thin, custom-made shells that are bonded to the front surface of teeth. They can effectively improve the appearance of teeth with issues such as stains, chips, cracks, or gaps. Veneers are often made from porcelain or composite resin and provide a natural-looking and durable solution.
  • Dental Bonding: Dental bonding involves the application of a tooth-colored composite resin material to repair chipped, cracked, or discolored teeth. The resin is shaped and bonded to the tooth, improving its appearance and restoring its function.
  • Dental Crowns: Crowns, also known as caps, are tooth-shaped restorations that cover and encase damaged or severely decayed teeth. They restore the appearance, strength, and function of the tooth, while also enhancing its aesthetics.
  • Dental Implants Dental implants are a long-term solution for replacing missing teeth. They involve the placement of an artificial tooth root (implant) into the jawbone, which serves as a stable foundation for a crown or bridge. Dental implants provide a natural-looking and permanent solution for restoring a complete smile.
  • Orthodontics (Braces or Clear Aligners): While orthodontics primarily focuses on correcting the alignment of teeth and jaws for functional reasons, it also has significant cosmetic benefits. Braces or clear aligners can help straighten crooked teeth, close gaps, and improve overall smile aesthetics.
  • Gum Contouring: Also known as gum reshaping or tissue sculpting, gum contouring is a procedure to reshape or reposition the gum line. It is often performed to correct a "gummy smile" or to create a more balanced and aesthetically pleasing gum-to-tooth ratio.
  • Dental Contouring: Dental contouring, also called tooth reshaping, involves removing small amounts of tooth enamel to alter the shape, length, or surface of the teeth. It is commonly used to smooth rough or irregular tooth edges, correct minor chips, or reshape uneven teeth.
  • Smile Makeovers Smile makeovers involve a comprehensive assessment and combination of cosmetic procedures to enhance the overall appearance of the smile. This may include a combination of teeth whitening, veneers, crowns, orthodontics, and other treatments tailored to the patient's specific needs and goals.

  • Tooth Extractions: Oral surgeons perform tooth extractions for various reasons, including impacted wisdom teeth, severely decayed or damaged teeth, or teeth that need to be removed to facilitate orthodontic treatment. They may also extract teeth in preparation for denture placement.
  • Dental Implant Placement: Oral surgeons are often involved in the surgical placement of dental implants. This procedure involves inserting artificial tooth roots (implants) into the jawbone to provide a stable foundation for prosthetic teeth or dental restorations.
  • Jaw Surgery (Orthognathic Surgery): Orthognathic surgery corrects abnormalities of the jaw bones and bite alignment. It may be performed to address issues such as malocclusion (misaligned bite), TMJ disorders, facial asymmetry, or to improve overall facial aesthetics.
  • Bone Grafting: In cases where the jawbone lacks sufficient volume or density to support dental implants or other oral prosthetics, oral surgeons can perform bone grafting procedures. Bone grafts involve adding bone or bone-like material to the jaw to enhance its strength and structure.
  • TMJ (Temporomandibular Joint) Disorders: Oral surgeons diagnose and treat conditions affecting the temporomandibular joint, which connects the jawbone to the skull. Surgical options for TMJ disorders include arthrocentesis, arthroscopy, and open joint surgery.
  • Corrective Jaw Surgery for Sleep Apnea: Oral surgeons can perform corrective jaw surgery to treat obstructive sleep apnea. The procedure involves repositioning the jaws to improve airflow and reduce obstruction during sleep.
  • Facial Trauma and Reconstruction: Oral surgeons are skilled in treating facial injuries, such as fractures of the jaw, cheekbones, nose, or orbits (eye sockets). They can perform surgical procedures to repair and reconstruct the affected areas.
  • Cleft Lip and Palate Repair: Oral surgeons work as part of a multidisciplinary team to repair cleft lips and palates in infants and children. These surgeries help improve speech, feeding, and overall facial appearance.
  • Biopsy and Treatment of Oral Pathologies: Oral surgeons can perform biopsies of suspicious oral lesions or growths to diagnose oral cancer or other pathologies. They may also be involved in the surgical removal or treatment of these conditions.

  • Dental Implant Placement: Implant dentists surgically place dental implants into the jawbone. The procedure involves making an incision in the gum tissue, drilling a hole in the jawbone, and inserting the implant. The implant integrates with the bone through a process called osseointegration, providing a stable foundation for the prosthetic tooth or teeth.
  • Single Tooth Implant: When a patient is missing a single tooth, an implant can be placed in the empty socket, effectively replacing the missing tooth root. Once the implant integrates with the bone, a dental crown is attached to the implant, restoring the appearance and function of the natural tooth.
  • Implant-Supported Bridges: Implant-supported bridges are used to replace multiple adjacent missing teeth. The implants are strategically placed in the jawbone, and a bridge is attached to the implants, effectively filling the gap left by the missing teeth.
  • Implant-Retained Dentures: Implant-retained dentures provide a more secure and stable alternative to traditional removable dentures. Implants are placed in the jawbone, and dentures are attached to the implants using specialized connectors or attachments. This prevents the dentures from shifting or slipping during speaking or eating.
  • All-on-4 or All-on-6 Implant Treatment: All-on-4 or All-on-6 treatments are innovative solutions for patients who have lost all or most of their teeth. These techniques involve placing a small number of dental implants (usually four or six) in each jaw arch to support a full set of fixed dentures. This approach provides a functional and aesthetic replacement for an entire arch of missing teeth.
  • Implant-Supported Overdentures: Implant-supported overdentures combine the stability of dental implants with the convenience of removable dentures. Implants are placed in the jawbone, and the dentures are attached to the implants using various mechanisms, such as ball attachments or a bar attachment system.
  • Implant Restoration: After the dental implants have integrated with the jawbone, the implant dentist will take impressions and fabricate the final dental prosthetics, such as crowns, bridges, or dentures. These restorations are custom-made to fit onto the dental implants and match the natural appearance of the surrounding teeth.
  • Implant Maintenance and Follow-up: Implant dentists provide ongoing care and maintenance for dental implants. This includes regular check-ups, professional cleanings, and monitoring the health of the implants, gums, and surrounding teeth. They may also address any concerns or complications that may arise.
